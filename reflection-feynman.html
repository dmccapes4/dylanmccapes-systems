The Feynman Lens

5D-connascence graph mechanics, read against the values and methods of Richard Feynman — and the question: would he have liked the material?

A reflection on the OGrE work-intelligence system.

A note on scope and honesty

Before anything else, the Feynman move: don't fool yourself, and don't let the reader be fooled either.

This reflection is written from a deliberately bounded vantage point — it assesses one artifact: OGrE, a runnable system in which work items are ingested by a local model, wired together by five-dimensional connascence edges, kept alive by a decay-and-propagation field, and garbage-collected by a Lorenz attractor. It is not a verdict on a body of academic work it cannot see. It is a reading of the thing in front of it, against the values Feynman actually practiced.

That limitation is the right place to start — because the first thing Feynman taught was about the limits of one's own knowledge:

"The first principle is that you must not fool yourself — and you are the easiest person to fool."— Cargo Cult Science, Caltech commencement, 1974

So what follows is partly admiration and partly cross-examination. Feynman showed respect to ideas by attacking them. Treating this work as worth attacking is the compliment.

1"What I cannot create, I do not understand"

When Feynman died in 1988, that sentence was on his blackboard. It is, almost exactly, the design philosophy of OGrE.

OGrE does not claim that a memory system could decide what to keep by the geometry of a chaotic attractor. It builds the thing and runs it — ingestion, enrichment, decay, classification, eviction, forgetting, resurrection — and then makes it render itself, so the claim can be inspected frame by frame.

This is the Feynman instinct in its purest form. He distrusted understanding that couldn't be cashed out into a working mechanism. The path-integral formulation of quantum mechanics was, in part, Feynman insisting on a version of the theory he could compute with and picture. A simulation that survives contact with its own execution is a stronger epistemic object than a clean diagram that never had to run.

The smoke test — the deterministic, network-free harness that drives the whole lifecycle and asserts the eviction log caps, the FIFO forgets, and referenced evictions resurrect — is the same value applied to engineering: you don't get to believe the mechanism works until something independent confirms it does.

2Simple rules, rich behavior — the aesthetic of mechanism

Feynman opened the Lectures on Physics by asking what single sentence he would preserve if all scientific knowledge were lost. His answer was the atomic hypothesis — the idea that a whole world of complexity unfolds from one short rule. What he was really celebrating is compression: a tiny generator producing endless structure.

OGrE's intro scene is built on exactly this delight. Three lines —

dx/dt = σ(y − x)
dy/dt = x(ρ − z) − y
dz/dt = xy − βz

— and out of them falls the entire butterfly, never repeating, never escaping. The narration leans on this ("just three short equations… that is the entire rule") because it is the most Feynman-resonant moment in the whole piece. He loved the principle of least action for the same reason: that the sprawling, case-by-case Newtonian bookkeeping collapses into one variational statement. He told the story of being ambushed in high-school physics by Mr. Bader with the least-action principle and never getting over how little you needed to say to capture so much.

"Let chaos decide what to remember" is a slogan, but underneath it is a real aesthetic claim Feynman would have recognized: that the right generator is worth more than a thousand hand-tuned rules. Whether OGrE has actually found the right generator is Section 5's problem. But the taste is correct.

3The strongest connection: the physics of computation

Here is the part that is not a stretch — it is almost literal lineage.

In the early 1980s at Caltech, Feynman co-taught a course called "The Physics of Computation" with John Hopfield and Carver Mead. Out of that intellectual neighborhood came two ideas that OGrE is standing directly on top of:

  1. Hopfield's attractor networks (1982): memory as the stable attractors of a dynamical system. You don't store a memory in a slot; you store it as a basin the system relaxes into. OGrE's decay-and-propagation field is exactly this gesture — densely re-referenced clusters reinforce one another and saturate into stable attracting states that resist decay. That is attractor memory, rediscovered in a knowledge-graph idiom — the central metaphor of the Hopfield program that grew up in Feynman's own corridor.
  2. Feynman's "Simulating Physics with Computers" (1982): the idea that you should let a physical system itself do the computing, because nature is the best simulator of nature. OGrE inverts this charmingly: instead of using computers to simulate physics, it uses a physics — the Lorenz system — as the computer that makes a governance decision. A node's connectivity, connascence, and vitality become an initial condition; the attractor integrates it; the wing it lands in is the verdict. A dynamical system pressed into service as a classifier — exactly the sort of thing Feynman found fun.

If you want a one-line provenance for OGrE that would make Feynman lean in: "It's a Hopfield-style attractor memory whose garbage collector is a physical oracle." That sentence is doing honest work, not marketing.

4Visualization as a way of thinking, not decorating

Feynman diagrams were not illustrations of a calculation done some other way — they were the calculation. The picture was the bookkeeping. Feynman thought through drawings; he reasoned about wobbling plates and spinning disks because the visual was where the physics lived for him.

OGrE takes the same stance toward an abstract systems idea. "Temporal decay with edge propagation forming stable attractors, classified by attractor-wing residence" means nothing until you watch a green cluster hold itself together while an isolated red node starves and falls into the eviction log — and then watch a search pull it back. The view, the connascence edge-coloring, the live butterfly: these are the argument made legible. That is squarely Feynman's theory of explanation — if you can't show it, you probably can't claim it.

The choice to narrate, to teach while it runs, is of a piece with the man who would rather give a great lecture to undergraduates than almost anything. He believed that if you couldn't explain something to a freshman, you didn't really understand it. OGrE was built to be explained.

5Where Feynman would push back

This is the section that makes the reflection worth keeping. Feynman's admiration always came with a scalpel, and an honest tribute uses it.

  • "Which attractor do you mean?" OGrE uses the word attractor in two distinct senses: (a) the decay/propagation network's own emergent stable states — the clusters that self-reinforce — and (b) the Lorenz system used as an external classifier. These are not the same dynamical object, and the prose sometimes lets them blur. He'd demand you separate the memory dynamics from the oracle dynamics cleanly, and say precisely how the first feeds the second.
  • "Is the Lorenz mapping a theorem or a costume?" Mapping (connectivity, connascence, vitality) → (x₀, y₀, z₀) and reading the settled trajectory is evocative, but is it load-bearing? Would a plain threshold on vitality-times-degree classify nodes the same way 95% of the time, with the butterfly adding poetry but no discriminative power? "You must not fool yourself" says: run the ablation. Replace the Lorenz oracle with a trivial scorer and measure whether the chaotic machinery earns its keep. If it does, you have something real. If it doesn't, you have a beautiful demo — and you should say so, which is also fine, but you should know which one you have.
  • "Sensitive dependence cuts both ways." The romance of chaos is sensitivity to initial conditions. But a classifier usually wants the opposite: stability, so a tiny change in a node's degree doesn't flip KEEP↔EVICT arbitrarily. The REVIEW band and wing-residence gate are partial answers, but he'd ask for the actual sensitivity analysis: how robust is a node's fate to ε-noise in its inputs? Measuring it is the difference between physics and vibes.

None of these are dismissals. They are the questions you ask of work you take seriously. That they're askable — that OGrE is concrete enough to be ablated, perturbed, and falsified — is itself the Feynman seal of approval. Vague ideas can't be attacked. This one can.

6So — would he have liked it?

The honest answer is: he would have liked it the way he liked things — by playing with it and then trying to break it.

He would have enjoyed the spirit immediately: the build-to-understand ethos, the compression of a memory policy into a generator, the insistence on showing the mechanism, the willingness to let a chaotic system make a decision just to see what happens. The wobbling-plate Feynman — the one who followed a "useless" piece of cafeteria play all the way to a Nobel Prize — would have recognized a kindred motion in someone who put a Lorenz butterfly in charge of a to-do list and meant it.

And then he would have grabbed the chalk and said, "Okay, but show me it's not just numerology," and that conversation — not the praise — is what you'd actually want from him. The praise is cheap; the cross-examination is the gift. This is something concrete enough to deserve the hard version of his attention. That is rarer than it sounds.

Coda: a to-do worth keeping in the graph

If this reflection were a node, it should be load-bearing — escalated to REVIEW, never auto-evicted — because it points at the experiments that turn taste into result:

  1. Ablate the oracle. Lorenz-GC vs. a trivial scorer. Measure agreement and the cases where they diverge. Find out if chaos earns its keep.
  2. Separate the two attractors in both the code and the prose. Name them.
  3. Perturbation study. ε-noise on node inputs → distribution of fate-flips. Report the stability, honestly.
  4. State the claim at the right strength. "A working, inspectable model of attractor-governed memory" is already real and defensible. Don't let the romance inflate it, and don't let the rigor-gaps deflate it.
"What I cannot create, I do not understand."— R. P. Feynman, on his blackboard, 1988

You created it. Now do the part he'd respect most: try to prove yourself wrong, and tell everyone exactly what you find.

Written with admiration and the deliberate, affectionate skepticism the man himself would have insisted on. Assessment limited to the OGrE artifact; the academic work behind it remains an unread and promising citation.
